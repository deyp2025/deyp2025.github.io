Compact Sets

Chapter 3: Basic Topology of $\mathbb{R}$  •  Spring 2026
📄  Download PDF version

Throughout our study of analysis, we have worked with sequences in infinite sets such as $(0,1)$, $\mathbb{R}$, etc. The concept of a compact set is designed to impose enough structure so that infinite processes can be controlled as if they were finite.

Motivating Question

Suppose $(a_n)$ is a sequence that does not converge. Can we guarantee some "convergent part" of it?

Recall the Bolzano–Weierstrass Theorem: every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence.

  • Does $a_n = (-1)^n$ converge?       Does it have a convergent subsequence?    
  • Does $a_n = n$ have a convergent subsequence?       Why not?
  • If $(a_n) \subseteq [0,1]$, must subsequential limits also lie in $[0,1]$?    

The key feature of $[0,1]$: it is bounded (so B-W applies) and closed (so limits stay inside). This is exactly what compactness captures!

Definition of Compactness

Definition 1

A set $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is called compact if every sequence $(a_n)$ contained in $K$ has a subsequence $(a_{n_k})$ that converges to a limit also in $K$: $$(a_n) \subseteq K \;\Longrightarrow\; \exists\; (a_{n_k}) \text{ with } a_{n_k} \to L \in K.$$

Compactness is a "self-contained" property: convergent subsequences exist, and their limits remain trapped inside $K$.

Open Covers and the Heine–Borel Theorem

Definition 3

An open cover for $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is a collection of open sets $\{O_\lambda\}$ with $A \subseteq \bigcup_\lambda O_\lambda$. A finite subcover is a finite subcollection whose union still contains $A$.

Example

Let $A = (0,1]$ and $O_n = \left(\frac{1}{n}, 2\right)$ for each $n \in \mathbb{N}$.

  1. Show that $\{O_n\}$ is an open cover of $(0,1]$.
  2. Can any finite subcollection $\{O_{n_1},\ldots,O_{n_k}\}$ be a subcover for $A$?
  3. Is $(0,1]$ compact? Why or why not?
Theorem 3 — Heine–Borel

Let $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. The following are equivalent:

  1. $K$ is compact: every sequence in $K$ has a subsequence converging to a limit in $K$.
  2. $K$ is closed and bounded.
  3. $K$ has the finite cover property: every open cover of $K$ has a finite subcover.
